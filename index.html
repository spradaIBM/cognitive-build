Meet PETTY and GETZABETH



  • Uncertainty

    Petty gave birth to a baby Girl named Getzabeth in 1988, she soon was diagnosed with Cerebral Palsy (CP) at age 1. CP is one of the most common causes of chronic childhood disability. It affects 10K infants each year. Causes are still unknown but evidence supports problems during, and immediately after birth.

  • Commitment

    Just like many parents, Petty became the main caregiver for her child. Family members are the main caregivers in most of the cases, they lack medical expertise and need to educate themselves about different types of therapies. Sometimes, they’re limited to immediate actions when an emergency situation arises. However, they put their heart and soul into this unpaid job.

  • Challenge

    Thinking. Learning and Feeling.Understanding Getzabeth can be challenging. Since Getzabeth was diagnosed, Petty has been in charge of her constant therapies: Physical, Occupational and Speech therapies to improve Getzabeth’s quality of life. Petty has also learned how to act in case of an emergency. Yet, Petty feels she lacks medical training and that she can still learn more about therapies and how to improve them for Getzabeth’s well being.


The Challenge:

More than 34 million unpaid caregivers provide care to someone who is ill or has a disability (AARP, 2008).

83% of caregivers are unpaid family members,
with no medical background and no previous experience on the field.

The Solution:

Supporting caregivers with a cognitive expert at home: Watson.

Watson Learns

Watson can learn from experience and read about a patient’s disabilities to help provide a better healthcare for the patient. It can provide effective routines for both the caregiver and the patient.

Watson Explains

Watson can inform the caregiver how to act in case of an emergency and give direct instructions on how to handle while waiting for medical assistance. Watson can then explain to the Doctor everything that has happened to a patient.

Watson Connects

WatsonCare can connect caregivers to support each other, share experiences and learn from others.

What makes it possible

As Watson becomes more knowledgeable in healthcare, it can provide technical support on treatments, therapies (physical, speech, occupational, psychological), medical history, and follow ups to help caregivers who take care of a patient with Cerebral Palsy or similar diagnosis, where the patient is unable to communicate properly.
Additionally Watson can help support caregivers by providing a support network of caregivers facing similar challenges.

To Keep in Mind

Autism

In America, one in 68 kids has autism, according to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. Most kids are diagnosed with autism between 2 and 6 months old, studies have found, and it tends to impact boys more than girls.

Cerebral palsy

Cerebral palsy occurs when the brain's development is slowed or damaged and a person can no longer function well physically, according to WebMD. It usually develops in kids when they are 2 or 3 years old, and affects more than 10,000 infants every year, WebMD reported.

Spina bifida

According to the Spina Bifida Association, spina bifida is one of the most common disabilities for children in the United States as eight babies every day are born with the split-spine disability. This requires kids to use wheelchairs, crutches or even braces to move around.

DOWN SYNDROME

The National Down Syndrome Society reported that Down syndrome affects one in every 691 kids every year, with 400,000 Americans overall having the disability. It occurs when parents pass on an extra copy of chromosome 21, leading to extra material in the brain. Down syndrome usually leads to some physical issues, like having a smaller stature and slanted eyes. But what happens to Down syndrome kids from a mental syndrome differs from kid to kid.

EPILEPSY

Across the world, 65 million people have been diagnosed with epilepsy, including 2 million in the United States, according to the Epilepsy Foundation. Epilepsy is a brain development disability that causes people to have seizures. A common way to spot epilepsy is if someone has two or more seizures within a 24-hour period, according to the Epilepsy Foundation.

Intellectual disability

This is a broad term to describe those who may have some mental limitations in what they can learn and understand. According to the National Dissemination Center for Children with Disabilities, intellectual disabilities can impact the way kids think, talk and walk. Some with intellectual disabilities can eventually learn things like other kids, but it takes more time and effort to try to understand everything they learn.

Watson Meets PETTY and GETZABETH

Every CP case is different. The needs are different:

Watson makes it possible to provide Petty with more contextual recommendations
to make her caretaking more effective.


  • Learn

    Watson learns about Getzabeth and her medical history and everything we know about CP. Watson can learn a patient’s medical record and keep track of the condition. It also learns and analyzes all previous and recent scientific literature as it becomes available about the medical condition diagnosed.

  • Advice

    Petty asks questions to Watson as Getzabeth’s condition shows new developments.Watson understands natural language and can also provide answers to basic and more advanced questions to provide advice and insight to assist new diagnosis based on a patient’s new developments.

  • Care

    Getzabeth’s vitals are fed to Watson to be monitored and tracked.Watson monitors and is in charge of emergencies, medication reminders and medical history tracking. Should any emergency be detected, it can provide help and even contact emergency services if necessary. It can also provide advice and crisis managements before, during and after crisis has taken place.

Watson now knows Getzabeth and Petty and now helps them to live the better life they always dreamed of.

Watson provides personalized, expert help by leveraging Getzabeth’s medical records, up to date scientific literature and patterns among similar cases worldwide to help answer basic and more advanced questions not only to Patty, but anyone caring for Getzabeth at any point during her life.
